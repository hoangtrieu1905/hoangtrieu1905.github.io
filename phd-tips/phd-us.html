PhD Application in the US
How it works, especially for Maths

*Any reference to “US institutions/ universities” is under the restriction to the Math/ Applied Math program of that school.

By reading this section, you will be able to answer the following questions:

  • How do I finance my PhD?
  • Can I apply to a PhD program right after my Bachelor/ Undergraduate?
  • Should I apply to a PhD program right after my Bachelor/ Undergraduate?

1. How do I finance my PhD?

Commonly, most schools pay you to be their PhD student, though it is not a guarantee in this funding cut era. Your PhD admissions usually comes with a financial aid package covering your tuition fees (excluding administrative fee, and this is different from school to school), health insurance (either waive or subsidize), and stipends (funded through research funds, teaching appointment, or fellowship - aka free money). You don’t make a lot of money, but hopefully enough to cover your living expenses so that you can focus on your research. PhD a hybrid experience - you are a student and a school’s employee with a full time appointment.

However, I can’t say this is true for all schools in the US. I will keep repeating this, but please reach out to schools before you apply for their PhD program. Overcommunicating is better than uninformed assumptions.

2. Can I apply to a PhD program right after my Bachelor/ Undergraduate?

When I told my family in Vietnam that I got a PhD right after my Bachelor, the common question is: “How is that possible?”

Yes, it is possible, and most people I know start their PhD program right after getting their Bachelor degree. Unlike in Vietnam or some European countries, most US institutions* allows direct admissions without a Master degree. Requirement and program structure differs from school to school, but in general, it will take 4-6 years to finish a PhD program in Math. People will take the first 2-3 years for courseworks, then after passing their Qualifying exams and Candidacy exam, they will get their Masters and officially advance to be a PhD candidate. They will then spend the rest of their time doing research and defending their PhD dissertation before getting their PhD. This process is not universal, and more information can be found on schools’ websites.

Now, another question is…

3. Should I apply to a PhD program right after my Bachelor/ Undergraduate?

Yes… if you know you want to work in academia. That’s what most people would suggest. My experience is a bit more than that. Getting a PhD does not mean (and guarantee) that you will work in academia, especially in this economy. Lots of UMD’s alumni that I connected with on LinkedIn work in the industry after their PhD.

Joining a PhD program is a huge commitment - you spend another 4-6 years in school doing research, taking courses, and teaching. You should ask yourself:

  • Beyond just post-PhD work experience, am I passionate about Math/(or any other fields) enough so that I could eat with it, sleep with it, swim in it, make my whole identity about it (well… this is a bit dramatic, but who knows) for the next few years? If you are passionate about it such that you want to totally focus on it, then you have your answer.
  • That being said, you might not have that kind of passion, but as long as you feel like you won’t get bored with it, then sure you can “try.”

That’s a lot of words. I had many reasons when I was applying for my PhD, but if I have to tell you my reason shortly, it would be because I love the math community and its people, and I would love to be a part of it. Very cliche, but that’s my reason.

Back to the big question: Should you apply to a PhD program right after your Bachelor/ Undergraduate? … No, if you just get the PhD for the sake of not knowing what to do after graduation. Getting a Masters degree would take less commitment so that you have more time to experience your interested fields, but at the same time I’m not sure if Masters have “good” funding like PhD programs. My old roommate did in fact get 3 fully funded Master programs in Geology though, so it never hurts to directly contact the program you were interested in.

That being said, your experience is unique. Anyone’s experience is. Therefore, take this section as a suggestion, not a strict guide. Maybe your story would be different, and if so, would you mind sharing it?
