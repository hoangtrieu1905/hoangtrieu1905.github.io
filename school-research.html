School & Advisor Research Strategies

In a PhD program, the specific university name matters far less than **your advisor**. You are entering an apprenticeship; having a supportive, well-funded advisor whose research interests align directly with yours will define your graduate experience.

1. Where to Find Advisors

Instead of searching university homepages alphabetically, use a bottom-up research strategy:

  • **Follow the Papers:** Look at the bibliography section of papers you enjoyed reading during your research experiences. Who authored them? Where do they teach?
  • **Litmaps & Citation Networks:** Use tools like [Litmaps](https://app.litmaps.com/) or Google Scholar to trace co-authorship networks around your research topics.
  • **Talk to Current Mentors:** Ask your current undergraduate research advisors. The mathematical community is highly connected; they likely know who is actively researching in specific fields.

2. Compiling the Research Spreadsheet

Create a structured database (Google Sheets or Excel) containing the following fields for each program:

  1. **University Name & Location**
  2. **Department / Program Name** (e.g., Applied Math, Computer Science)
  3. **Primary Advisor(s)** (Name 2–3 faculty members per school)
  4. **Admissions Deadline** (Usually early Dec)
  5. **GRE Requirement** (Required / Optional / Not Considered)
  6. **Application Fee & Waiver Details**
  7. **Notes on Recent Publications** (Key papers, funding availability)

"Do not apply to a university simply because it is high in general rankings. If there is only one professor at that institution who works in your area, and they leave or do not take students that year, you will be stranded."

3. Evaluating "Advisor Fit"

Advisor fit consists of three distinct pillars:

A. Research Fit

Does the advisor work on topics you are qualified and excited to study? Review their recent preprints (last 2–3 years) on arXiv or personal homepages to confirm they are still active in the field.

B. Funding Fit

Does the advisor have active NSF/NIH/DoE research grants? If an advisor is well-funded, they can hire you as a Research Assistant (RA), exempting you from teaching duties to accelerate your dissertation.

C. Mentorship Style Fit

Does the advisor prefer weekly hands-on meetings, or do they give students complete independence? During interviews, ask the advisor's current students about their work environment to assess work-life balance.
