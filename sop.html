Writing the Personal Statement & SOP

The Statement of Purpose (SOP) is your main narrative document. It connects the bullet points on your CV into a logical, forward-looking story detailing what you did, why you did it, and what you plan to accomplish in graduate school.

1. SOP vs. Personal Statement: What is the Difference?

Many universities ask for both documents. Mixing them up is a common mistake:

  • **Statement of Purpose (SOP):** A strictly professional research proposal. It answers: What research questions excite you? What projects have you completed to prove you are ready to tackle them? Which faculty members do you want to work with and why?
  • **Personal / Diversity Statement:** A document explaining your personal history. It answers: What challenges (financial, systemic, geographic) have you overcome? How does your unique background contribute to the diversity of the graduate student body?

2. Structure of a Strong SOP

A standard 2-page Statement of Purpose should follow a structured progression:

Paragraph 1: Intro & Core Interests

State clearly what program you are applying to and your primary areas of research interest (e.g., Numerical Analysis, Combinatorics, Partial Differential Equations). Avoid childhood clichés ("I have loved math since I was five").

Paragraph 2 & 3: Research Accomplishments

Detail 1–2 research projects. Use the **SCAR** structure: **S**ituation (what was the project goal?), **C**ontribution (what specific task did *you* perform?), **A**ction (how did you solve it?), and **R**esult (a presentation, paper preprint, or software library).

"Don't just state the math; explain your role in it. For example, explain how you refactored the C-RCWA solver code or derived the closed combinatorial bounds yourself."

Paragraph 4: School & Faculty Alignment

Explain why this specific school is an ideal fit. Name 2–3 faculty members whose recent papers relate directly to your research interests. Detail how your skills complement their active projects.

Common Mistakes to Avoid:
- **Too vague:** Saying "your university is highly ranked" without mentioning faculty members.
- **List-like:** Repeating your CV in prose format without adding context.
- **Unprofessional:** Spending too much time discussing bad grades or test scores. Keep the tone forward-looking and constructive.
